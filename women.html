Designing a spirited motion identity for Smithsonian Channel's new punk-feminist webseries

Designing for the moment

What unites great women in history? When presented with the challenge to unify female American scientists, artists, educators, and leaders, I found a palpable thematic and visual connection in a punk-feminist identity: the universal story of punching above your weight class.


As an exercise in not just motion design, but also editing, I first assembled hundreds of clips from shows for this episode featuring Annie Clark, a two-time Grammy winning singer-songwriter. The goal was to present a clean, thematically consistent array of motion that allowed me to transition smoothly between scenes.


For a "hero" animation, I edited and cut together concert stills to assemble an eye-catching and vibrant scene, keeping the viewer's eye.
I valued smoothly transitioning between scenes, while maintaining visual spectacle and interest. Typography-wise, I chose a combination of a thick, black-weighted typeface to create color blocks while contrasting with a condensed subface.


Into the spotlight! This quick transition was made to introduce segments of the content featuring the "rise" of the person. This could be used with footage, drawings, narration, etc. Typically, this allows me to smooth over cuts in narration. The editing will allow me to develop a linear narrative from several interview sessions or clips.


Part of the groundwork in this project involved going through footage and stills from concerts. I carefully weighed my options, edited and colored images, and composed them in a 2.5D (moving camera in After Effects) to build the sensation of "moving" through Annie Clark's timeline.


Moving footage and animation are key to building an impressive experience out of a short video--viewers will take keys from seeing just seconds of footage of playing, actors, etc.


Skills used:

Typography

Avenir Next Condensed
A bold, sans-serif typeface designed in the late 1900s, Avenir is a linear sans that has evolved from the stylistic developments of the twentieth century. I chose it as its thicker vertical strokes gave it a great appearance for headlines, and gave me ample room for distortion, editing, and motion use.


Futura
My use of Futura was inspired by its use in popular culture, such as the Watchmen comics. I chose a condensed weight while still tracking it in, using the text as a color blocking element.


Druk
Druk is a highly versatile font capable of filling any screen in an engaging way--I use it's less extreme, condensed version so as to hit accents throughout the motion piece that remain legible.


Image Treatment

In experimenting with concert footage and provided photography, I found a load-bearing element of Women Who Make America's' motion identity! Using a combination of image distortion, lower framerates, and gradient mapping (pink-yellow) I found an eye-popping combination of shifts to bring all the material from this series into a consistent theme.
