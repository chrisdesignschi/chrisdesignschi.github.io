Beneath the Surface for Multiple Sclerosis

2019 / Advertising Campaign / Nonprofit

MS is an invisible disease.
The symptoms are debilitating, but hard to describe or see.

During my internship at Razorfish Health, the Multiple Sclerosis Association of America (MSAA) asked us to create a year-long creative ad campaign to raise awareness + funds for their Swim for MS program. Against 80+ other Publicis Groupe interns, my pitch won!


Beneath the Surface is about showing the unseen truths of Multiple Sclerosis.

My goal was to prime online users to associate swimming with MS, using iconic swimming gear to represent common symptoms of the disease. These animated illustrations also allowed me to excite a younger audience and show the truth of MS better than any combination of stock photos and text.

"I am not great like everyone thinks I am. But do you know what is great?
Finding someone who knows that you aren't."

Reddit user Sonoallie, diagnosed with relapsing-remitting MS

My ethnographic research of both MS patients and Gen Z found that they shared one common trait: frustration over not being understood when it came to unseen feelings. I created a collection of case studies from Reddit and Facebook online communities, as well as Google Search trends data.

Instagram sponsored story advertisements, with the goal of educating and empathizing.

Swim for MS is a DIY fundraiser. That can be daunting. In collaboration with our linguistics intern, I created a flowchart that would make it easier for individuals to set up events with the tools they already had.

Some bonus collateral I created for the client, in a merchandise proposal.

Superdry's Breathing Retail Website

2018 / Coding, Interaction / Retail Fashion

I love the Superdry fashion brand.
I wanted to redesign and code their retail site.

My research showed Superdry's competitors succeeded by frequently engaging customers at a low-level online versus massive seasonal campaigns. I surmised that online, Superdry might improve sales the same way—thus, I built a site that focused on weekly engagement.

Click here to see the fully-coded site on my Github!

I knew that both the store locator and the cart would be the most important interactions on the site. Thus, I took cues from Superdry competitor sites to create UI blocks with a natural hierarchy encouraging users to proceed with purchases.

The new hero section, on both desktop and mobile, has 5 key elements: a headline, subhead, hero graphic, social link, and call to action.

My research showed that competitor streetwear brands like Supreme spiked in popularity during collaborations—for example, Google Trends showed mutual growth each time Nike and Supreme released a collab. Thus, I planned for my new site to take as much advantage of Superdry's collaborative works.

Additionally, streetwear brands proved far more successful when they "drip" products, usually on a weekly basis. I proposed that Superdry release clothing online on a more frequent basis, rather than through large collections that might fall to short attention spans.

My vision for the new site included a degree of playful interactiveness that encouraged users to explore the site, and see the new sales/drops on a daily basis.

Public Health: Knowing the Opioid Crisis

2017-19 / Data Visualization, Motion Design / Public Health

Opioid addiction is a complex and worsening crisis.
I've led volunteers in raising awareness and training civic partners for 2 years.

In Chicago, the introduction of fentanyl, a drug identical in appearance to heroin yet many times stronger, has ravaged low-income communities. In addition to the body of work created by my volunteer group for the Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH), I've also created a series of solo work dedicated to informing and preparing the general public to do good in their own communities.

I started with data. The Annual Opioid Surveillance Report, composed by the Office of Epidemiology and Research, is used to track the severity of opioid crisis and allows for officials to speak accurately to the state of their communities.

Before designing anything, I studied dozens of ethnographic data tables supplied by the CDPH. This allowed me to highlight correlations between variables like prescription vs illegal drug usage, income-level, and types of drug.

I also included data taken from the Cook County Medical examiner. Despite Chicago's reputation for gun violence, the data shows that opioid overdoses take far more lives.

An important distinction to make is Chicago's unique severity regarding opioids—rates of death in Chicago are far higher than in the rest of Illinois.

I also found that the most at-risk demographic for opioid overdose death was 40-50 year old African American men in low-income brackets.

An important function of the Opioid Surveillance Report is to dispel misinformation. I found that the opioid misuse was not a race-specific problem; rather, an income issue.

Fentanyl is a new drug identical to heroin in powdered form, which leads to accidental ingestion and hundreds of deaths annually. This was the focus of my report.

With this graph, I hoped to show that while heroin still made up the bulk of opioid deaths in 2017, fentanyl had nearly surpassed it in severity within just a year.

In addition to the Opioid Surveillance Report, I also produced a 2-minute motion design video made to quickly train new CDPH employees or volunteers on the benefits of Naloxone, an overdose-reversing drug.

Redesigning SAIC's Education Infrastructure

2018 / User Interaction Design, Prototyping / Education

Nearly all of SAIC's digital infrastructure is accessible by desktop only. Here's my mobile-first take.

Being a student requires use of many desktop-only sites to access buildings, class schedules, and pay fees. More than that, these infrastructures are split across 3 platforms: Canvas, Self Service, and Engage.

I wanted to create a mobile app that would cover it all.

Try the Sketch interactive prototype here

Shows the tasks required to navigate the app. I tried to group all my functions under a maximum of 3 tabs, which you can easily cycle between. Forms are shown in pop-up form.

Use states were important to me, as I knew I would be designing an app with lots of functions on one page; as such, I needed users to know exactly how to navigate a screen.

Additionally, I built a modern visual library that matched the school's colors and brand as an institute for fine art.

Onboarding
My sign-in page is simple, colorful, and enhanced by Leroy Neiman's (SAIC alum) bright artwork. I additionally animated a loading screen based on the school logo.

Adding Funds
Financial transactions via the SAIC page requires passing through 4 levels of navigation. While adding cards and securing transactions is a lengthy process, I minimized it by making it feel like it was one fluid interaction.

Booking Room Reservations
Room reservation at SAIC is done via the Engage platform — I wanted to merge it into the same app as other similar functions. The barcode is taken from the student card, available at all times for easy access.

Primer Mood-Changing App

2019 / User Interface, User Experience Design / Mobile App

In psychology, "priming" is the theory of previous stimuli affecting future performance.​

I wanted to create a fun app that someone might use before a stressful interview, speaking in public, or anything else where "priming" or some kind of preparation would help you. I researched case studies to create an app that uses color + motion to help.

I had a lot of fun using Adobe XD's "auto-animate" feature to simulate the background changing as you use the UI — even if you never played the game, I imagined just using the app as being a calming experience.

The "creative" game that I programmed — clicking changes the color of the dot, and also leaves behind a smaller and slowly shrinking dot behind it. I envisioned this as being a fun, short, game that one could play to get their mind primed for creative thinking.

The "calm" game that I programmed — you're given 5 dots of varying sizes, and your interactions slowly but surely affect their size and color. This could be something you fiddled to distract yourself from stress.

The "amp game — my favorite. I created a simple dot that was falling off the screen, and clicking it postpones the inevitable "game over". I encouraged users to go faster by having the dot increase in size momentarily after being clicked, making it easier to go at speed.
